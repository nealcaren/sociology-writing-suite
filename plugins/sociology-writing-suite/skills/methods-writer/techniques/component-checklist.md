# Component Checklist

This guide specifies what to include for each component of a Methods section. All examples are drawn from the 77-article corpus of *Social Problems* and *Social Forces* interview studies.

---

## 1. Case Selection / Study Rationale

### What to Include

| Pathway | Required | Optional |
|---------|----------|----------|
| **Efficient** | Brief population mention | -- |
| **Standard** | Population + setting | Brief case justification |
| **Detailed** | Full case selection logic | Unit of analysis; comparison rationale |

### How to Phrase It

**Efficient (minimal)**:
> "I draw from 41 interviews with Black middle-class adults."

**Standard (brief justification)**:
> "This paper draws on 47 interviews conducted with guest mothers in Chicago between 2019 and 2021. Chicago provides an important case given its high rates of housing instability and diverse demographic composition."

**Detailed (full logic)**:
> "The six cases provide an ideal set of comparisons for several reasons. First, they experienced mobilization during the same period. Second, they share similar political opportunity structures. Third, they vary systematically in the variable of interest."

### Example Sentences from Corpus

**Why this case**:
- "an important place to study given its [characteristic]"
- "this is an important opportunity to examine..."
- "uniquely suited for investigating"
- "provides an ideal set of comparisons"

**Why interviews (not surveys)**:
- "inadequate for identifying the discursive and cognitive processes"
- "surveys are prone to social desirability bias"
- "unable to disentangle the sequential relationship"

## 2. Sample Size and Composition

### What to Include

| Pathway | Required | Optional |
|---------|----------|----------|
| **Efficient** | N; basic demographics in prose | -- |
| **Standard** | N; demographics (prose or table) | Table recommended |
| **Detailed** | N; table required; population comparison | Response rates |

### How to Phrase It

**Sample N**:
> "In total, 47 participants were interviewed."
> "We draw from 71 multigenerational in-depth interviews."
> "This study is based on repeated interviews with 51 people."

**Demographics in prose** (Efficient):
> "The sample was majority male (87 percent) and white (55 percent). Respondents ranged in age from 28 to 64."

**Demographics with table reference** (Standard/Detailed):
> "Table 1 presents selected characteristics of our participants."
> "As shown in Table 1, all respondents are Black, and most are women (64 percent)."

**Population comparison** (Detailed):
> "Relative to the population of [group], our sample over-represents women and under-represents those without college degrees."
> "Sample racial demographics were generally reflective of the county jail population."

### Example Sentences from Corpus

**Direct statements**:
- "In total, X participants were purposively sampled"
- "The data include interviewees who range in age from..."
- "Most respondents were racial minorities (Black, X percent; Latino/a Y percent)"

**With breakdown**:
- "X interviews with [group A] = N individuals; Y interviews with [group B] = M individuals"
- "In total, X intervieweesâ€”Y from [site A] and Z from [site B]"

## 3. Recruitment Strategy

### What to Include

| Pathway | Required | Optional |
|---------|----------|----------|
| **Efficient** | Named approach | -- |
| **Standard** | Named approach + channels | Eligibility criteria |
| **Detailed** | Approach + channels + process | Response/refusal rates |

### Named Approaches

- **Purposive**: "purposively sampled"
- **Snowball**: "snowball sampling"
- **Convenience**: "convenience sampling approach"
- **Stratified/Random**: "a stratified random sample was drawn"
- **Combination**: "purposive and snowball sampling techniques"

### How to Phrase It

**Efficient**:
> "To find middle-class participants, I used purposeful snowball sampling, recruiting from Black alumni associations and professional organizations."

**Standard**:
> "Participants were recruited through personal contacts, recruitment ads on social media, and subsequent snowball sampling. To qualify, participants must have [eligibility criteria]."

**Detailed**:
> "Recruitment took place in several stages. The first group included [group A], recruited via [channel]. We then interviewed [group B], identified through [process]. In total, 215 [population] were asked to participate; 75 refused, resulting in a final sample of 140."

### Example Sentences from Corpus

**Channels**:
- "recruited from a number of organizations, some of which actively engaged in [activity]"
- "recruiting from Black alumni associations of large universities, Black faculty organizations, campus staff groups"
- "Several nonprofit organizations advocating for immigrant rights supported our recruitment"

**Gatekeeper-mediated**:
- "Local partners facilitated access to [group A]. [Group A] facilitated access to [group B]"
- "The Los Angeles County Sheriff's Department provided the research team with bi-weekly lists"

**Response rates**:
- "X interviewees refused, resulting in a final sample of Y"
- "The youth response rate for this study is just over X percent"

## 4. Interview Protocol

### What to Include

| Pathway | Required | Optional |
|---------|----------|----------|
| **Efficient** | Duration | Topics (brief) |
| **Standard** | Duration; topics; recording | Location; compensation |
| **Detailed** | All of above; instrument provenance | Sample questions |

### Duration Reporting

**Range only**:
> "Interviews ranged from 45 minutes to two hours."

**Range with average**:
> "Interviews lasted from one to three hours; the median was 97 minutes."

**By participant type** (Detailed):
> "Parent interviews averaged 1 hour; youth interviews ranged from 40 minutes to 2.5 hours."

### Topics Covered

**Efficient** (brief):
> "Interviews covered family experiences and housing decisions."

**Standard** (listed):
> "The interview guide asked questions about: 1) career pathways; 2) organizational experiences; and 3) professional identity."

**Detailed** (with sample questions):
> "We began with broad questions such as 'Can you tell me about your family?' and then asked specific questions about [topic]. Youth interviews began by asking each respondent to 'describe a typical day.'"

### Recording and Transcription

**Standard formula**:
> "All interviews were audio recorded and transcribed."

**With exceptions**:
> "All but two interviews were audio-recorded and transcribed; detailed notes were recorded by hand for the remaining two."

### Location

> "Interviews were conducted at libraries, cafes, and in participants' homes or offices."
> "Interviews took place in person while they were confined in [facility]."

### Compensation

> "Respondents were paid $25 in cash for each interview."
> "Participants received a $30 gift card."
> "Adults received a $50 gift card and children received a $25 gift card."

### Language

> "Interviews were conducted in English or Spanish, depending on the respondent's preference."
> "I employed Spanish-speaking translators to accompany me in five parent interviews."

## 5. Analysis Approach

### What to Include

| Pathway | Required | Optional |
|---------|----------|----------|
| **Efficient** | Named approach with citation | Software |
| **Standard** | Approach + citation + software + process | Intercoder reliability |
| **Detailed** | All above + specific codes | Prevalence statistics |

### Named Approaches with Citations

**Abductive**:
> "Following an abductive approach (Timmermans and Tavory 2012)"
> "I coded and analyzed the transcripts using abductive techniques (Tavory and Timmermans 2014)"

**Grounded theory**:
> "I used grounded theory methods (Charmaz 2006; Strauss and Corbin 1990) with open, axial, and selective coding"

**Thematic**:
> "Initially coded following an inductive thematic approach (Braun and Clarke 2006)"

**Flexible coding**:
> "We used flexible coding procedures, as outlined by Deterding and Waters (2018)"

### Software

> "Interviews were coded using Dedoose software."
> "Interview transcripts were analyzed in ATLAS.ti."
> "We imported transcripts and fieldnotes into NVivo for coding."

### Coding Process

**Efficient**:
> "Following an abductive approach (Timmermans and Tavory 2012), I coded using Dedoose."

**Standard**:
> "Analysis involved a two-step process. Initial coding revolved around reading each transcript in its entirety and developing broad thematic codes. This was followed by a second step of more focused line-by-line coding."

**Detailed**:
> "Open coding was first employed to identify initial concepts. These codes included *trust, rejection, loneliness, shame, hopelessness, excitement*, among others. The remaining transcripts were then coded deductively using these theoretically-driven codes."

### Intercoder Reliability (Standard/Detailed, if team)

> "We monitored inter-coder reliability throughout by meeting frequently to discuss the coding process."
> "All transcripts were coded by at least two research team members and reviewed on a weekly basis."

## 6. Positionality

### When to Include

| Pathway | Include When |
|---------|--------------|
| **Efficient** | Rarely (only if essential) |
| **Standard** | Interviewer-respondent mismatch; identity shaped access |
| **Detailed** | Encouraged, especially with vulnerable populations |

### What to Include

1. **Identity disclosure**: Who are the researchers?
2. **Potential constraints**: How might identity have limited data?
3. **Potential benefits**: How might identity have helped?
4. **Mitigation/interpretation**: What does this mean?

### How to Phrase It

**Brief identity disclosure**:
> "All three interviewers identify as white, and two as female."
> "Both researchers interviewed participants. We are both immigrants."

**Constraining effects**:
> "May have restricted the stories our interviewees felt able to tell."
> "Queer men of color likely spent considerable time explaining their particular challenges."

**Enabling effects**:
> "May have elicited a particular desire to justify the meritocratic nature of one's success."
> "Participants may have viewed my comparative privilege as an opportunity to communicate their experiences to a broader audience."

**Mitigation**:
> "I directly and generously quote respondents as a way to ensure that the sociological claims reflect the lived experiences of those most affected."

## 7. Ethical Considerations

### When to Include

- Vulnerable population (incarcerated, undocumented, survivors)
- Non-standard consent (verbal, waived)
- Information security measures
- Topics deliberately not asked

### What to Include

**IRB**:
> "The study received institutional review board approval."

**Consent** (if non-standard):
> "We obtained verbal rather than written consent and did not maintain records of personal information."

**Information security**:
> "Potential participants contacted us through a dedicated cellphone line or an encrypted email account."

**Topics not asked**:
> "We did not ask for details regarding a participant's current or past charges."

**Support provision**:
> "Each family also received a resource packet with information about housing, legal, and food assistance programs."

---

## 8. Confidentiality Statement

### When to Include

- Efficient: Required (brief)
- Standard: Recommended
- Detailed: Optional (ethics section may cover)

### How to Phrase It

> "For the sake of confidentiality, all respondents' names have been changed."
> "To ensure confidentiality, I have used pseudonyms throughout."
> "Participants were given pseudonyms."
