# Sentence Toolbox

A vocabulary of sentence-level moves for Theory section writing, based on analysis of 80 articles from *Social Problems* and *Social Forces*.

---

## 1. Opening Sentence Types

Theory sections and paragraphs begin with distinct sentence types that signal rhetorical function. Select appropriately.

### 1.1 Literature-Announcing Openers (25-30% of paragraphs)

Signal entry into an established scholarly conversation.

**Signature patterns**:
- "A growing body of research..."
- "Over the past [X] decades, [scholars/sociologists] have..."
- "Research on [topic] has..."
- "An emerging literature examines..."
- "A burgeoning literature documents..."

**Examples**:
> "A growing body of research examines the ways migrant children and their parents back home manage their relationships."

> "Over the last three decades, cultural sociologists have developed a rich theoretical and empirical research platform to demonstrate how culture works in a variety of institutional contexts."

> "In the past two decades, sociologists have demonstrated the persistence of systemic oppression along the lines of race, class, sex, gender, sexuality, and other social locations."

**Best for**: Opening synthesis paragraphs, beginning the theory section.

---

### 1.2 Context-Setting Openers (20-25%)

Establish empirical phenomena, historical developments, or social conditions.

**Signature patterns**:
- "As [phenomenon] has [developed/expanded/changed]..."
- "The [adjective] [phenomenon] has..."
- "In recent [decades/years]..."
- "With the [expansion/rise/decline] of..."

**Examples**:
> "As housing costs have outpaced wage growth, many parents face difficulty housing their families."

> "The dramatic reconfiguration of the U.S. state over the past four decades has generated a body of criminological scholarship centered on the unprecedented magnitude of carceral expansion."

> "The US immigration system is situated within a global regime of borders."

**Best for**: Opening the theory section, introducing substantive context.

---

### 1.3 Theory-Introducing Openers (15-20%)

Explicitly name a theoretical framework, concept, or scholar.

**Signature patterns**:
- "The concept of '[X]' draws on..."
- "[Framework/Theory] posits that..."
- "According to [Scholar]..."
- "[Scholar's] (Year) concept of [X]..."
- "Central to this analysis is..."

**Examples**:
> "The concept of 'recognition' draws on several conceptual traditions."

> "The procedural justice model asserts that people make fairness evaluations according to four process-based criteria."

> "Connell (2005) conceptualizes hegemonic masculinity as the most honorable expression of manliness within a hierarchy of masculinities."

**Best for**: Theory-Extension and Concept-Builder articles, framework exposition paragraphs.

---

### 1.4 Gap-Marking Openers (10-15%)

Immediately signal what is missing or understudied.

**Signature patterns**:
- "Yet we know little about..."
- "However, [scholars/research] has not..."
- "What remains unclear is..."
- "Largely unexplored in studies of [X] are..."
- "Despite [X], we have limited understanding of..."

**Examples**:
> "Yet while research to date provides insight into *why* residents persist in calling on law enforcement, there is less attention to the cultural scripts through which people themselves seek to *make sense* of the institutions with which they engage."

> "Largely unexplored in studies of transnational elder care are the experiences of adult children who remain in the place of origin with their aging parents."

**Best for**: The "turn" paragraph, after synthesis.

---

### 1.5 Contrast-Signaling Openers (10-12%)

Introduce tension between competing positions or shift analytical direction.

**Signature patterns**:
- "However, [alternative view]..."
- "In contrast to [X], [Y]..."
- "While [X], [Y]..."
- "Others, however, argue..."
- "At the same time..."

**Examples**:
> "The study of feminist social movements provides a contrasting account of the mobilization of civil society into carceral activities."

> "Others, however, argue that a tendency toward conspiratorial thinking is not indicated by psychological predispositions as much as indicative of a 'cognitive style.'"

**Best for**: Debate-framing, presenting alternative perspectives.

---

### 1.6 Definition-Providing Openers (5-8%)

Define key terms, often introducing newly coined concepts.

**Signature patterns**:
- "[Concept] refers to..."
- "I use the term [X] to..."
- "We define [X] as..."
- "[Concept] is..."
- "By [X], I mean..."

**Examples**:
> "I use the term *carceral creep* to suggest the incremental and often imperceptible advance of carceral forces."

> "*Work primacy* refers to the centrality of work in the economic and noneconomic lives of undocumented immigrant youth."

> "We define recognition as the 'fact of being acknowledged' through the institutional provision of services, validation, and dignity."

**Best for**: Concept-Builder articles, new terminology introduction.

---

### 1.7 Claim-Making Openers (5-8%)

Make direct argumentative assertions about the study's contribution.

**Signature patterns**:
- "I argue that..."
- "This study [examines/considers/extends]..."
- "We contend that..."
- "This paper [integrates/demonstrates/shows]..."

**Examples**:
> "I argue that Du Bois offers an integrated framework for understanding immigrants' incorporation strategies."

> "This paper integrates procedural and distributive justice models in order to examine how both legal treatment *and* outcome experiences influenced respondents' perceptions of (in)justice."

**Best for**: Stating your contribution, previewing the argument.

## 2. Transition Marker Inventory

### 2.1 Additive Markers

Connect supporting or elaborating ideas.

| Marker | Usage |
|--------|-------|
| "Building on" | "Building on this work, scholars have..." |
| "Moreover" | "Moreover, CPS may be a meaningful presence..." |
| "In addition" | "In addition to documenting..." |
| "Similarly" | "Similarly, research on procedural justice..." |
| "Likewise" | "Likewise, researchers have shown..." |
| "Furthermore" | "Furthermore, individual property owners..." |
| "Also" | "They also maintain boundaries along..." |
| "Extending this insight" | "Extending this insight, we see..." |

### 2.2 Contrastive Markers

Signal disagreement, limitation, or alternative perspectives.

| Marker | Usage |
|--------|-------|
| "However" | Very high frequency; primary turn marker |
| "Yet" | Very high frequency; often begins gap sentence |
| "In contrast" | "In contrast to [X], [Y]..." |
| "Despite" | "Despite making important strides..." |
| "Although" | "Although earlier evaluations showed..." |
| "While" | "While Kanter articulated the challenges..." |
| "Nevertheless" | "Nevertheless, transnational fathers increasingly..." |
| "Rather than" | "Rather than show a clear progression..." |

### 2.3 Temporal Markers

Situate developments in historical context.

| Marker | Usage |
|--------|-------|
| "In recent [decades/years]" | Very high frequency |
| "Over the past [X] decades" | High frequency |
| "Historically" | "Historically, one strategy people use..." |
| "Since the [1960s/1990s]" | "Since the 1960s, responding to..." |
| "Today" | "Today, mandated reporters typically..." |
| "Increasingly" | "Increasingly, scholars have acknowledged..." |

### 2.4 Causal/Consequential Markers

Signal logical implications.

| Marker | Usage |
|--------|-------|
| "As a result" | "As a result, outsiders must strategically navigate..." |
| "Thus" | Very high frequency; "Thus, hyper visibility also limits..." |
| "Therefore" | "Therefore, even a tenant approved..." |
| "In turn" | "Mode of entry, in turn, shapes..." |
| "Given" | "Given these concerns..." |

### 2.5 Exemplifying Markers

Introduce specific illustrations.

| Marker | Usage |
|--------|-------|
| "For example" | Very high frequency |
| "For instance" | "For instance, outsiders tend to be evaluated..." |
| "Such as" | "...such as 'stop-and-frisk,' hot-spot policing..." |
| "As [Scholar] demonstrates" | "As Okamoto's foundational research demonstrates..." |

## 3. Hedging Calibration

### When to Hedge (may, might, suggests, can)

- **Theoretical claims about mechanisms**: "This process may operate through..."
- **Predictions about findings**: "We expect that..."
- **Generalizations beyond the sample**: "These dynamics may apply to..."
- **Causal claims without experimental evidence**: "X may lead to Y"

### When to Assert (demonstrates, shows, establishes, is)

- **Reporting prior literature**: "Studies demonstrate..."
- **Defining concepts**: "Recognition refers to..."
- **Gap statements**: "We know little about..." (assertive about the gap)
- **Research questions**: "This study examines..." (unhedged)

### Hedging Vocabulary Spectrum

| Strong Hedge | Moderate Hedge | Moderate Assertion | Strong Assertion |
|--------------|----------------|-------------------|------------------|
| "may" | "suggests" | "indicates" | "demonstrates" |
| "might" | "tends to" | "shows" | "establishes" |
| "could" | "often" | "finds" | "confirms" |
| "perhaps" | "typically" | "reveals" | "is" |
| "possibly" | "appears to" | "illustrates" | "clearly" |

## 4. Cluster-Specific Sentence Patterns

### Gap-Filler
- Heavy **context-setting** and **literature-announcing** openers
- **Contrastive** markers ("however," "yet") for the turn
- **Efficient parenthetical** citation strings
- Moderate hedging, **assertive gap statements**

### Theory-Extender
- **Theory-introducing** openers with named theorists
- "According to [Scholar]"; "Building on"; "Drawing on"
- **Author-subject** citation pattern dominant
- Lower hedging on established theory

### Concept-Builder
- **Definition-providing** openers for new terminology
- Causal chains ("as a result," "thus")
- **Quote-then-cite** for detailed engagement
- Low hedging on conceptual definitions

### Synthesis Integrator
- **Literature-announcing** and **theory-introducing** mixed
- Bridging phrases ("bringing together," "connecting")
- Citations from multiple traditions juxtaposed
- Moderate hedging on integration claims

### Problem-Driven
- **Context-setting** dominant (50%+ for Empirical sub-type)
- **Contrast-signaling** for debates ("some argue," "others contend")
- Temporal markers establishing historical context
- Higher hedging on competing explanations

## Quick Reference: Sentence Starter Bank

### Starting a Synthesis Paragraph
- "A growing body of research examines..."
- "Over the past [X] decades, scholars have..."
- "Research on [topic] demonstrates that..."

### Starting a Theory Paragraph
- "The concept of [X] draws on..."
- "[Scholar] (Year) theorizes [concept] as..."
- "Central to this framework is..."

### Starting the Turn
- "Yet we know little about..."
- "However, less attention has been paid to..."
- "What remains unclear is..."

### Stating Your Contribution
- "This study examines..."
- "I/We argue that..."
- "This paper integrates..."
